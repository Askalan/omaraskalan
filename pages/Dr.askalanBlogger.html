THE HISTORY OF SOMALI AGRICULTURAL PRODUCTION 1970-1980.

“Most of Somali young agricultural engineers are interested to be agronomist” Why Agronomy? Somalia is the world's largest frankincense producer. Historical background of agricultural production practices in Somalia Cultivated crops into Somalia go back, probably in colonial times. There is Strong belief that Italians, Arabs, and Indians introduced crops in Somalia for their daily needs and to a lesser extent for their daily income purposes. Agricultural production practices and crop handling grew among the Somalis after 1960. Agriculture is the most significant sector of the economy, accounting for four times the value of agricultural production and earning almost three-fifths of Somalia's international transactions. In Somalia, agriculture is categorized into three subsectors.
More 1. the nomadic pastoralism is first one, which occurs outside of agricultural regions. This sector, which focuses on; goats, sheep, camels, and cattle, and it has grown more market oriented. 2. The second sector is the traditional farming system, chiefly subsistence, agriculture practiced by small farmers and limited areas. This traditional sector is divided into two types farming systems: a. Rain-fed farming in the south and northwest that grows sorghum, usually with a huge livestock herd. If these farmers get government support can product these products: butter, cheese, cream, yogurt, ghee, condensed milk, dried milk, ice cream, etc. and produces various by-products including buttermilk, whey, ghee, and skim milk. Unfortunately, they can only depend on animals’ exportation, to maintain their daily life. b. Small, irrigated farms that plant crops; maize, sesame, cowpeas, vegetables, and fruits. These farmers are not near rivers, but they can irrigate crops by taking water from long distance rivers or near lakes, also well that is 10-30 m depth. These farmers still have faith to support the people lost their resource due to drought that occurs continuously in the country. 3. The third sector consists of medium-scale market-oriented farming and large-scale irrigated land along the lower Jubba and Shabelle rivers. There are the major crops: bananas, sugarcane, rice, cotton, vegetables, grapefruit, mangoes, and papayas. The acacia species of southern Somalia's thorny savanna provide fine timber and are the main source of charcoal, although charcoal output has long beyond environmentally permissible limits. More efficient and cautious management of frankincense, myrrh, and other resin-producing plants may boost aromatic gum production. In the north, the country's minor fishing industry centers around the catch and canning of tunny (tuna) and mackerel. Sharks are frequently captured and dried by artisanal inshore fishermen. Choice fish and shellfish are prepared for export in southern Somalia. Climate change, overfishing, and increased piracy along Somalia's coasts harmed the country's fishing economy in the early twenty-first century.
